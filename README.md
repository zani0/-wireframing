## ğŸ§¾ Types of Wireframes

Wireframes come in different levels of fidelity, depending on the stage of the design process and the intended audience. The two most common types are **low-fidelity** and **high-fidelity** wireframes.

### 1. âœï¸ Low-Fidelity Wireframes

Low-fidelity wireframes are simple, sketch-like representations that focus on structure and layout without detailed content or styling.

**Characteristics:**
- Uses placeholders (e.g., boxes for images, lines for text)
- Often black-and-white or grayscale
- Minimal detail and interactivity

**When to Use:**
- Early in the design process to brainstorm ideas and explore layouts
- For quick feedback and iterations
- In team discussions or stakeholder presentations to focus on concepts rather than visuals

**Example Use Case:**
- Mapping out a homepage layout with a header, hero section, content blocks, and footer using rough shapes.

---

### 2. ğŸ¨ High-Fidelity Wireframes

High-fidelity wireframes are more detailed and closely resemble the final product in terms of content, layout, and interactivity.

**Characteristics:**
- Includes actual content (or realistic placeholders)
- Shows detailed visual hierarchy and typography
- May simulate interactivity (clickable buttons, dropdowns, etc.)

**When to Use:**
- In later design stages to finalize layouts and content positioning
- Before moving into prototyping or development
- For presenting to clients or stakeholders who need a clearer vision of the final product

**Example Use Case:**
- Designing a detailed wireframe for a login page with labeled input fields, button states, and validation messages.

---

Both low-fidelity and high-fidelity wireframes serve unique and important purposes. Choosing the right type depends on your goals, audience, and where you are in the product design journey.

## ğŸ“š Real-World Scenario: Catching Usability Issues Early

### Scenario: Redesigning a University Course Registration System

A university planned to redesign its outdated course registration portal. The team began with wireframes to visualize the new student dashboard, course catalog, and registration flow.

### Identified Issues During Wireframing:

During a stakeholder review of the **low-fidelity wireframes**, a major usability issue emerged:

- The **"Add Course" button** was buried at the bottom of each course detail page, making it hard for students to locate and use efficiently.
- The **flow for checking prerequisites** required users to navigate to a separate page, causing unnecessary back-and-forth.

### How the Issues Were Resolved:

- The design team **moved the "Add Course" button to the top-right corner**, making it easily accessible and consistent across pages.
- They introduced a **prerequisite popover**, which allowed users to view and verify requirements inline without leaving the page.

These fixes were implemented at the **wireframe stage**, before any coding had begun, saving the team considerable development time and effort.

### Impact on the Final Product:

- The redesigned interface led to a **40% decrease in support requests** related to course registration.
- Students reported a **smoother, more intuitive experience** during usability testing and post-launch feedback.
- The development team was able to **build the solution faster** since layout decisions had already been finalized.
